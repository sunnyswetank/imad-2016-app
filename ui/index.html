Hello, World!

Welcome to the Sunny side of the street

This is my very first (and certainly not the last) web app. A big shout out to the IMAD team for their support during this course. A course that provided a great overview of the web app development process and of the various components that form a typical web app. The examples, illustrations and practical modules are easy to follow. In fact, I have utilized many of the practical modules illustrated in the course to build this website

What you see here is an attempt to build on the knowleddge gained through the course and make a simple web app that tells a story - its own story. It's a meta app!

Who am I?

Like really?

I am a Data Miner by profession. I work with raw datasets (e-commerce transactions, product reviews, travel bookings etc.) to find actionable insights. Most of these insights are predictions with an associated probability or confidence level. Take the example of a revenue management project for a hotel. I may be asked to predict the demand for a given day or I may have to assess the likelihood that a customer will cancel his or her booking. If I am working with an e-commerce client, the client may be interested in finding the best product recommendations for their customers. On the other hand, a bank may want to discover a set of customers from its existing customer base, who are most likely to adopt a recently launched premium travel credit card.

I am sure you are familiar with some of these problem statements and use cases. Very often, to present the results of my analysis, I visualize and highlight the most important results. For analysis, I generally use a mix of R, SAS, Hive (on Hadoop) and for the visualization and presentation I may use PowerPoint or Tableau (usually when a live dashboard needs to be built).

Birth of the cool

What does this webapp do?

The highlights of the app are listed below. Click on each panel to read more!

A menu bar on the top listing links to the various sections of the web app. This bar is fixed at the top. The menu elements change color on hover.

Click on any menu option to go to the relevant section on the same page.

On scroll, the section moves to the top of the page just below the menu bar. This transition takes the same amount of time each time a menu option is clicked.

The page renders suitably for various screen sizes. For example, the long navigation bar collapses into a drop down menu. Or the padding is designed differently for various screen sizes.

The page consists of a comments box. Visitors can leave comments here.

The database stores all comments added by users immediately. All comments displayed are powered by the database.

A log in functionality for visitors to register themselves on the app

Show number of likes, dislikes for comments and provide option of sorting comments by date, likes etc.

Life of a hunter-gatherer

In pursuit of appiness

From the list in the previous section, I realized that the top half of the features are geared towards serving static content and the lower half to serving dynamic content, using a significant inputs from the users. I decided to finish the static section first - including the writing of this content that you are presently reading.

I used jQuery to highlight the selected sections and for the page scroll transitions. I started off by looking for solutions for these problems on the Internet and realized that using jQuery was probably the easy answer, since that was the predominant approach in most of the answers. I did a quick course in jQuery on codeacademy to understand basic syntax and functions.

After some trial and error with bits of code from different places (mostly answers to problems on stackoverflow), I was able to achieve my objectives with few lines of code in jQuery. You can find them in the source of this page. I have added comments for convenience. Once I had the menu selection and section navigation piece working, I spent some time on enhancing readability and aesthetic appeal (as much as my limited design sensibilities would permit)

The perfect is the enemy of the good!

Counting stars

The feature list in the earlier section talks about only those features that I could implement in the current version. For clarity, I have not inlcuded the wish list of features that I set out with. So, this section lists some of the features that I wanted to work on but had to shelve as they were too ambitious or proving to be a road-block.

The comments section currently is an iframe. I want to have the comments page as part of the main HTML page but am yet to figure out how to do it.

While I am partial to the neutral white background with the splash of teal in certain sections, I also wanted to use some gradients and images as backgrounds for a couple of sections. I found a great website named Pixabay. This site allows you to download images and save them on your hosting server for free usage. Unfortunately, I could not get the images to show up in the IMAD console for referencing.

I feel the text on this page is not easily readable when compared to text heavy websites such as THe Guardian, The Atlantic or Edge.org. I tried to look at their source code and styling sheets but I could not recreate the effect. I noticed that the text body on these websites is rather limited to less than half the width of the screen (true for laptop and desktop screens). I tried to do the same but without images or gradients on the side margins, horror vacui kicked in.

The comments section is rather primitive and serves the basic function of being able to store comments and serve them through a database. I wanted to add some intelligence in the form of real-time metrics (most commented/funniest/longest/shortest etc.) and tagging (pro/against/neutral) through a bit of text mining. That would broughh this web app closer to CMSs like livefyre or DISQUS.
